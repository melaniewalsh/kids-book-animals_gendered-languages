Animal Gender: Language Face-Off

How do we gender animals in different languages?

By Melanie Walsh

I recently published a story with The Pudding called “Bears Will Be Boys,” a data analysis of how people assign gender to anthropomorphic animals. We read 300 popular English-language children’s books from the past 70+ years. Across all these books, we found a few clear patterns.

Frogs were overwhelmingly boys. Birds were girls. Pigs leaned a little male but were slowly moving into their Girl Boss era.

Our findings sparked a heated discussion on social media, especially among those who speak languages beyond English. Many people perceived these animals differently.

“Really funny to read this as someone speaking French, a Language Where Everything is Gendered,” one commenter said. “Obviously birds are hes and frogs are shes, what do you mean (haha).”

It’s a great point. Language absolutely shapes the way we imagine animals. This may be especially true for Languages Where Everything Is Gendered—aka languages with grammatical gender, where nouns (including animals) are assigned a masculine, feminine, or sometimes neuter gender.

What’s grammatical gender?

Grammatical gender is a system found in many of the world’s languages where nouns—words for things like animals, objects, and people—are sorted into categories like masculine, feminine, or sometimes neuter. These gender categories usually don’t have anything to do with biological sex or social gender. A lamp (la lampe) is grammatically feminine in French, even though a lamp doesn’t have a gender in real life.

Here are a few quick examples from different languages. Notice how the article (“the”) or adjective (“grumpy”) shifts depending on whether the noun is grammatically masculine, feminine, or neuter:

🇫🇷 La grenouille est grognonne.

“The frog is grumpy.”

(grenouille is feminine; la and grognonne agree.)

🇩🇪 Der Vogel verspottet mich.

“The bird is mocking me.”

(Vogel is masculine; der is the matching article.)

🇮🇳 बिल्ली शायद पिशाच है। (billi shayad pishaach hai)

“The cat is probably a vampire.”

(billi, cat, is feminine in Hindi, so verbs and adjectives in full agreement would also reflect that.)

English, by contrast, doesn’t gender most nouns. A cat is just “a cat.” You say “the cat is grumpy” no matter what. English used to have grammatical gender. Old English—the language of the original Beowulf, which existed from around the 5th century to 1066—had masculine, feminine, and neuter nouns, just like modern German. But over time, English shed these categories. Today only pronouns like he and she remain gendered.

Many studies have shown that grammatical gender does influence people’s perception and associations. We acknowledged this point in our original story, but you made it clear: for many of you, grammatical gender really shapes your imagined sense of an animal’s gender, even if it’s a fictional animal character living in a two-story house.

So I wanted to do a quick breakdown of how 10 different languages with grammatical gender—Arabic, French, German, Greek, Hindi, Italian, Polish, Portuguese, Russian, and Spanish—represent 14 different animals. How do these animals match up? And how do they compare to our English-language findings?

Note on these categorizations

These categorizations are definitely open to interpretation and debate! There are many linguistic gray areas!

What animal words count in the “Varies” category?

I labeled animal words that can take either masculine or feminine forms—like el gato and la gata—as “Varies” because I wanted to distinguish them from words like la rana (frog), which are grammatically fixed as feminine. You can’t say el rano in Spanish.

But it’s fuzzy. While you can say la gata, the masculine form gato often functions as the default, especially when the cat’s sex is unknown or unspecified. So should that count as “Varies” or “Defaults to Masculine”? Reasonable people (and linguists) may disagree.

Which animal words did you choose?

There’s also the issue of multiple words for the same animal—formal, informal, poetic, regional. In Hindi, for instance, there are at least two common words for “bird”: पक्षी (pakshī), which is more formal or literary, and चिड़िया (chiṛiyā), which is more childlike and commonly used in everyday speech. That’s kind of like the difference in English between saying “canine” and “dog.” For this project, I tried to choose words that felt closer to what you’d find in children’s books, words with emotional or cultural weight in everyday language.

All of this is tricky, and I’m not a linguist or a speaker of most of these languages (except for my bad Spanish). So if you have thoughts, corrections, or better examples, I’d genuinely love to hear them.

Grammatical gender is not destiny

But, of course, grammatical gender is not destiny. And, in fact, it’s often very arbitrary (for example, in French, the word for vagina, le vagin, is grammatically masculine ¯\_(ツ)_/¯). So even if the word for an animal is grammatically feminine, it doesn’t mean that animal will always be represented as female—or, in the case of anthropomorphic characters, as a girl or a woman.

In Spanish, where frogs are always grammatically feminine (la rana), you still find dude frogs. The famous Muppets character Kermit the Frog is masculinized through the addition of a traditionally male name—La Rana René in Mexico and Latin America, or La Rana Gustavo in Spain.1

There are other ways of conjuring male frogs in Spanish, like using the closely related word el sapo (toad). The Spanish translation of Arnold Lobel’s famous Frog and Toad series even uses a totally made up word, sepo—Sapo y Sepo, inseparables—to describe the iconic frog in slacks and distinguish him from his sapo bestie.

While both these examples are famous male frogs imported from English, you see the same pattern with stories that originate in the same language.

Even though the German word for ladybug is masculine, you still find plenty of little girl ladybug characters.

Language, Man

Language, man. It’s trippy, isn’t it? Seeing people trade perceptions of animals on the internet is really brain-melty and fun. It felt like learning that people see a different color blue—destabilizing, but also exhilarating.

It’s the best and most liberating part of the internet, and of connecting with others, at least I think so.
