Animal Gender: Language Face-Off

How do we gender animals in different languages?

By Melanie Walsh

I recently published a story with The Pudding called “Bears Will Be Boys,” a data analysis of how people assign gender to anthropomorphic animals. We read 300 popular English-language children’s books from the past 70+ years. Across all these books, we found a few clear patterns.

Frogs were overwhelmingly boys. Birds were girls. Pigs leaned a little male but were slowly moving into their Girl Boss era.

Our findings sparked a heated discussion on social media, especially among those who speak languages beyond English. Many people perceived these animals differently.

“Really funny to read this as someone speaking French, a Language Where Everything is Gendered,” one commenter said. “Obviously birds are hes and frogs are shes, what do you mean (haha).”

It’s a great point. Language absolutely shapes the way we imagine animals, totally separate from reality or biology. And this may be especially true for Languages Where Everything Is Gendered—aka languages with grammatical gender, where nouns (including animals) are assigned a masculine, feminine, or sometimes neuter gender.

We acknowledged this point in our original story, but you made it clear: for many of you, grammatical gender really shapes your imagined sense of an animal’s gender, even when it’s a made-up character in a kid’s story.

So I wanted to do a quick breakdown of how 10 different languages with grammatical gender—Arabic, French, German, Greek, Hindi, Italian, Polish, Portuguese, Russian, and Spanish—represent 14 different animals. How do these animals match up? And how do they compare to our English-language findings?

Animal Face-Off

Let’s start with frogs. In the popular English-language children’s books that we examined, frog characters were overwhelmingly portrayed as male—94% of the time. In languages like Arabic, German, Greek, and Hindi, frogs are grammatically gendered male, too.

Feminine grammatical gender makes sense, since many words for ladybug have strong (and weird!) associations with the Virgin Mary, “God’s little cow,” and other feminine figures (for more on that, see our original story).

Grammatical gender is not destiny

But, of course, grammatical gender is not destiny. And, in fact, it’s often very arbitrary (for example, in French, the word for vagina, le vagin, is grammatically masculine ¯\_(ツ)_/¯). So even if the word for an animal is grammatically feminine, it doesn’t mean that animal will always be represented as female—or, in the case of anthropomorphic characters, as a girl or a woman.

In Spanish, where frogs are always grammatically feminine (la rana), you still find dude frogs. The famous Muppets character Kermit the Frog is masculinized through the addition of a traditionally male name—La Rana René in Mexico and Latin America, or La Rana Gustavo in Spain.1

And there are other ways of conjuring male frogs, like using the closely related word el sapo (toad). The Spanish translation of Arnold Lobel’s famous Frog and Toad series even uses a totally made up word, sepo—Sapo y Sepo, inseparables—to describe the iconic frog in slacks and distinguish him from his sapo bestie.

While both these examples are famous male frogs imported from English, you see the same pattern in stories from the same language.

Even though the German word for ladybug is masculine, you can still find plenty of little girl ladybug characters.
